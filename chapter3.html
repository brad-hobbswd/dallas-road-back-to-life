Chapter Three

The War Within

The loudest battles are often fought where nobody else can see.

Morning arrived quietly, yet inside Dallas, a storm raged. The sun rose gently, painting the sky in soft blues and lavender, but his mind moved in restless circles. He sat at the edge of his mattress, elbows on his knees, palms covering his face.

Last night had offered a glimpse of peace, yet the craving for drugs still pulled at him, insistent and familiar, like an old friend knocking on the door of his soul.

He tried to pray, but the words scattered. Opening his Bible, he read the verse again:

"A double minded man is unstable in all his ways."

The words followed him like shadows, echoing through every part of him. He knew he stood at a crossroad. One path led toward God and the life he had begun to sense. The other pulled him back into the shadows he knew all too well.

He rose and walked to the bathroom, staring at his reflection. His eyes were tired, but he no longer looked empty. Something had shifted deep inside him. He whispered to the man in the mirror: I want stability. I want clarity. I want freedom. I want Jesus to guide my heart and mind, fully and completely.

Dallas left his apartment and walked toward the old café by the river. The morning air was crisp. The streets were quiet. The scent of the water drifted through the cool air. He hoped a warm drink would ease the pressure building in his chest.

As he neared the café, he noticed a man sitting outside. Simple gray sweater, steady, gentle eyes. Dallas recognized him. It was Micah, the man who had prayed for him in the alley the night before, the man who had appeared just when he needed guidance. Micah looked up and smiled, calm and unforced.

Dallas approached cautiously. Micah nodded and gestured to the empty chair beside him.

"Sit with me, Dallas," he said.

Dallas hesitated, surprised Micah already knew his name, then sat. The chair felt cold beneath him, but Micah’s presence radiated warmth.

"I watched you fight last night," Micah said. "Not with your fists, but with your heart. That is the harder battle."

Dallas swallowed hard, feeling exposed. "I thought I was losing."

"You did not lose," Micah replied. "You turned toward life. That alone is victory."

Dallas gazed at the river flowing steadily beyond the café. It did not force its way. It simply moved where it was meant to move. He wished his life could flow with such grace.

"Why is this so hard?" he asked. "I want to choose God, but the other path still calls me."

Micah leaned back slightly. "Because you have walked that path for a long time. The familiar always speaks the loudest. But you must understand something: you are no longer fighting for survival. You are fighting for purpose."

Dallas exhaled slowly. "I feel torn."

"You are," Micah said. "A double minded heart cannot stand. But a heart fixed on Jesus becomes unshakable."

The words pierced him, reaching deep places he had kept locked away.

Micah continued, "You must decide which voice you will follow. The voice of your past tells you that change is impossible. The voice of Jesus tells you that you are already changing."

Dallas rested his hands on the table, feeling the weight of his choices press down on him. He knew Micah was right. He had followed the wrong voice for years. Every attempt to rise had been met with whispers of unworthiness.

"Micah," he asked carefully, "how do I keep choosing the right voice when the wrong one feels so loud?"

Micah’s eyes softened. "You do not choose with strength. You choose with surrender. When a man surrenders his whole mind to Jesus, he no longer stands alone. You cannot silence the wrong voice by yourself. But He can silence it for you."

Something loosened in Dallas’s chest. Breath moved freely for the first time that morning. He closed his eyes and let the words settle into him.

Micah stood and placed a gentle hand on his shoulder. "This is only the beginning. The war within you is real, but so is the One fighting for you."

Dallas opened his eyes. Micah smiled and began walking away, leaving him with a strange blend of peace and conviction.

He watched Micah disappear down the quiet sidewalk. When he looked back at the river, it reflected what he wanted his life to become: steady, clear, moving in the direction Jesus had set for him.

"I want to be single minded," he whispered.

The morning breeze brushed across his face, as if something unseen acknowledged his desire. Dallas rose and began to walk. He did not know exactly where he was going, but for the first time, he felt as though he was choosing the right direction.

A choice had been made, even if he did not yet fully understand it. And heaven had taken notice.
