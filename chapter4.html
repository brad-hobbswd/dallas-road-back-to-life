Chapter Four

The River of Relapse

Some currents pull backward. Grace teaches you how to keep moving forward.

The sun had risen higher, painting the sky in shades of gold and pink. Dallas walked along the edge of town, following a worn trail that led toward the river he had glimpsed from the café. The water shimmered in the morning light, smooth and inviting, yet tension pressed in his chest.

His steps faltered. The river reminded him of nights spent hiding, sneaking, and running from himself and the world.

He stopped at the riverbank and studied the water. Calm had often concealed treacherous currents. A small raft floated nearby, loosely tied to a post.

Memories of his past rose like a wave. Countless shortcuts. Temporary relief. The emptiness that always followed.

But the memories of his past rose like a wave. The countless shortcuts he had taken, the relief he had sought in all the wrong places, and the emptiness that had always followed. He swallowed hard, knowing temptation was waiting.

A small plastic bag floated on the water. His stomach twisted. He recognized it instantly; the same type of packaging he had once used for drugs. His heart raced. The temptation whispered, just one time. One time will take the edge off. You can handle it.

Dallas shook his head violently.

No. Not again.

He dropped to his knees on the riverbank, pressing his hands into the cold earth. His body trembled, fighting memory and pull alike. He closed his eyes and prayed aloud, his voice shaking.

God, help me. I am tired of this fight. I do not want to go back. Please make a way. Please give me strength.

A gentle wind stirred, carrying a calm that felt almost tangible. When Dallas opened his eyes, the raft and the bag seemed distant, as if the river itself had shifted. A warmth settled on his shoulder, invisible yet real, as if God's presence had wrapped around him.

He remembered Solomon's words from the valley. Freedom comes from surrender, not from trying to fight alone. Dallas realized he did not have to rely on his own strength. He could choose God one step at a time, allowing Him to carry the weight.

Taking a deep breath, Dallas rose and began walking along the riverbank, ignoring the raft and the bag. The water flowed steadily beside him, clear and unwavering. The river became more than water; the river became a reminder of his choice to keep moving forward. Life could flow steadily when God guided the direction.

After some time, he reached a narrow bridge crossing the river. The wood creaked beneath the slightest weight, yet the bridge remained firmly anchored, unlike the raft he had ignored. He stepped cautiously, feeling each board support him as he crossed.

Halfway over, memories surged. Hiding. Lying. Shame. Fear. Doubt pressed against him. He could stop. He could turn around. He could give in.

Dallas pressed his hands against the railing and whispered:

God, You are stronger than my cravings. You are stronger than my fear. I choose You.

The words echoed through him. Each step forward felt lighter. By the time he reached the opposite bank, Dallas felt a peace he had not known in years. He looked back at the river and imagined every mistake and every wound drifting away beneath the current.

He smiled, feeling strength that no longer belonged to him alone. God had carried him across, showing him that surrender could triumph over temptation.

Dallas continued down the trail into the hills. He understood the journey would not be easy. More rivers would appear. More valleys would come. More nights would arrive when old voices whispered louder than prayer. Yet he also understood something new.

He was not walking alone.

I choose You, Lord, he whispered again. I will keep moving.

The hills opened into a sunlit path and the breeze carried the faint scent of pine and wildflowers. Hope stirred within him. He had faced the river of relapse and survived. God had shown him that the old life could no longer hold him unless he allowed it.

For the first time, Dallas believed in the possibility of real freedom.
